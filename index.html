Rebuilding Dams in Suffolk County

Preamble

Many dams on Long Island were built decades ago for industrial, agricultural, or recreational purposes. However, changing environmental conditions, aging infrastructure, and evolving water management strategies have raised questions about whether certain dams should be rebuilt after destruction.

Open Letter to County Executive

Dear County Executive Edward P. Romaine,

I am writing to urge careful reconsideration of the proposed dam reconstruction efforts at Stonybrook Mill Pond and Stump Pond. While I understand the intention to restore these structures, I believe this approach poses significant risks to Long Island's ecological integrity and long-term sustainability.

Dams disrupt natural water flow, fragment aquatic habitats, and impede fish migration, leading to a decline in native species and overall biodiversity. In our region, species such as river herring and American eel rely on free-flowing waterways to complete their life cycles. Restoring natural stream connectivity would bolster these populations and enhance the overall health of our aquatic ecosystems.

Moreover, maintaining and reconstructing dams often results in costly, ongoing maintenance and liability concerns. As climate change exacerbates flooding and extreme weather events, aging infrastructure may become increasingly vulnerable. Removing the dams would not only alleviate these long-term financial burdens but also promote resilience by allowing rivers to adapt naturally to changing environmental conditions.

Finally, dam removal presents an opportunity to improve water quality and restore wetland ecosystems. Healthy wetlands act as natural buffers, filtering pollutants, mitigating flooding, and providing critical wildlife habitat. These ecosystem services are invaluable for ensuring the long-term environmental and economic well-being of Suffolk County.

I urge you to prioritize ecological restoration and sustainable water management practices over dam reconstruction. Doing so will safeguard our natural resources for future generations and position Suffolk County as a leader in conservation and environmental resilience.

Thank you for your time and consideration.

Sincerely,

Xavier Rubira

Background

This project focuses on the dams at Mill Pond in Stony Brook, New York, and Stump Pond in Smithtown, New York, which were destroyed in a storm in August 2024. The dam at Mill Pond has historically provided aesthetic and recreational benefits, but concerns about sediment accumulation and ecological disruption have emerged. Similarly, Stump Pond, a prominent feature supporting diverse wildlife, also raises issues related to water quality and hydrological alteration.

Key Considerations

  1. Environmental impact of rebuilding versus removal
  2. Hydrological changes and water quality concerns
  3. Effects on local wildlife and biodiversity
  4. Economic implications for Suffolk County
  5. Public and stakeholder interests
